# Unit 1 - Hinduism

## Diwali

1. All religious festivals connect ancient stories to modern rituals and involve communities gathering to remember, commit and worship.
2. The word "Diwali" means "Full of lights". This is why it is called the Festival of Lights.
3. The festival celebrates the victory of good over evil, light over darkness and knowledge over ignorance
4. Traditional diva lamps are small clay pots filled with ghee or oil
5. Hindu homes, shops and public places are decorated for Diwali. Lights are the main feature e.g. fairy lights and candles, as well as traditional diva.
6. Colourful patterns called rangoli can be seen on the doorsteps of homes to welcome visitors and good fortune in the shape of Lakshmi, the goddess of wealth.
7. Some Hindus leave windows and doors of their houses open so that Lakshmi can enter and grant good fortune.
8. Many Hindus start the new business year during Diwali.
9. + 10. The Festival often prepared and celebrated by:
- Cleaning the home
- Putting up decorations
- Cooking feast food, particularly Indian sweets and pudding
- Re-telling Diwali stories through drama, song, dance and art
- Wearing new clothes
- Exchanging gifts and cards
- Firework Displays

## Holi

1. Holi is known as the festival of colours and is celebrated during the spring
2. Hindus wear coloured clothes and throw coloured powders over each other, which covers them from head to toe.
3. The festival lasts two days and is in February/March at a time when the moon is full
4. The festival is a celebration of good over evil because Holika was burnt in a fire when she tried to kill Prahlad.
5. There are several stories associated with Holi. Some people believe the origin of the festival lies with Lord Krishna. In Hindu scriptures he is described as a very mischievous young boy. Stories include throwing colour water over the maids and even stealing their clothes while they were bathing in a river.
6. The practical jokes and paint fights, which are now a common feature of Holi, are thought to come from these stories.
7. The story of Prahlad symbolises good overcoming evil and is why bonfires are traditionally lit at Holi.
8. Prahlad refused to worship the King and worshipped God in the form of Lord Vishnu instead.
9. Ashes from Holi bonfires are thought by some Hindus to bring good luck.
10. Holi is an opportunity for some Hindus to think about the things they can learn from scriptue and they are reminded of the importance of staying true to their beliefs, as Prince Prahlad did.
